Executive Summary

This tabletop exercise simulates a coordinated distributed denial-of-service (DDoS) attack against Rainbow Six: Siege game infrastructure during peak evening hours. The attack combines volumetric network floods with application-layer attacks targeting game servers, authentication services, and CDN infrastructure. With over 200,000 concurrent players affected and social media backlash mounting, teams must coordinate rapid detection, mitigation, and communication while maintaining service availability.

Attack Vector
Multi-vector DDoS: UDP amplification (500+ Gbps), SYN floods, and Layer 7 HTTP floods targeting game matchmaking and authentication APIs
Potential Impact
Complete service outage affecting 200K+ concurrent players, revenue loss ($50K+/hour), player churn, reputation damage, potential ransom demands
Testing Goals
Validate DDoS detection and mitigation procedures, test CDN failover mechanisms, evaluate cross-team communication during high-pressure outages, identify gaps in player communication protocols
Critical Gaps
DDoS mitigation playbook for gaming infrastructure, automated CDN failover procedures, player communication templates during attacks, incident escalation thresholds

How to Run This Exercise

This guide provides everything you need to facilitate a successful tabletop exercise. Read through this section before the exercise day.

📋 Pre-Exercise Preparation

Timeline: Begin preparation 2-3 weeks before exercise

Tasks:
  • Book war room or large conference room with projector/screen
  • Send calendar invites to all participants (SOC, DevOps, Infra, GameOps, PR/Comms)
  • Confirm availability of exercise runner (facilitator assistant)
  • Prepare digital inject materials (fake monitoring dashboards, Twitter screenshots)
  • Set up shared document for real-time note-taking (Google Doc or Confluence)
  • Print evaluation forms and gap analysis worksheets
  • Test projector and screen sharing setup
  • Prepare backup plan if remote participants have connectivity issues
  • Review Rainbow Six architecture diagram with technical participants beforehand
  • Coordinate with PR team to prepare realistic social media mockups
Materials Needed:
  • This facilitator guide printed or on tablet
  • Laptop connected to projector for displaying injects
  • Monitoring dashboard screenshots (pre-prepared fake alerts)
  • Twitter/social media mockups showing player complaints
  • Rainbow Six infrastructure diagram (high-level architecture)

  • Gap analysis worksheets

Room Setup:
  • U-shaped or conference table seating (encourages discussion)

🎤 Opening Script

Good morning/afternoon everyone. Thank you for participating in today&#039;s tabletop exercise. I&#039;m [Your Name] and I&#039;ll be facilitating. This is a learning exercise, not a test - there are no wrong answers, and this is not a performance evaluation. The goal is to identify gaps in our processes and improve our incident response capabilities.

We&#039;re going to simulate a DDoS attack against Rainbow Six infrastructure. I&#039;ll present the scenario, then deliver timed &#039;injects&#039; - new information or complications. Your job is to discuss how you would respond as a team. Feel free to ask questions, request information, or disagree with each other - that&#039;s how we learn.

Let&#039;s establish some ground rules before we begin.

⚖️ Ground Rules to Establish

  • No wrong answers - this is a learning environment, not a test
  • Psychological safety - speak openly without fear of judgment or consequences
  • Stay in character - respond as you would in a real incident, not theoretically
  • No phones or laptops except for note-taking (minimize distractions)
  • Assume normal staffing levels and resources (no 'we'd hire a consultant' shortcuts)
  • Ask clarifying questions anytime - facilitator has additional information
  • Respect speaking time - everyone's input matters, even junior team members
  • Disagree productively - different perspectives help identify gaps
  • Document action items as we go - we'll use these in gap analysis
  • Scheduled break at 45-minute mark (bathroom, coffee, stretch)

📊 Exercise Flow Overview

The exercise is structured as a chronological incident timeline starting at T+0 (initial detection) and progressing through multiple phases: detection, investigation, mitigation, escalation, and communication. Each inject introduces new complications or information requiring team decisions. The attack will escalate in sophistication, testing your ability to adapt. Total timeline spans 2 hours of simulated incident time, compressed into our 90-120 minute exercise. We'll pause after each inject for discussion, then move forward when the team reaches consensus or needs more information.

Total Duration: 90-120 minutes
Time Breakdown:
  • Opening and ground rules: 10 minutes
  • Scenario overview and Q&A: 10 minutes
  • Inject sequence and discussion: 60-80 minutes (6 injects, ~10-12 min each)
  • Hot wash debrief: 15-20 minutes
  • Gap analysis and action items: 10-15 minutes

Note: If discussion is particularly productive on a key decision point, allow it to run longer. Better to cover fewer injects deeply than rush through all of them.

💡 Facilitation Best Practices

  • Create psychological safety - emphasize learning over performance evaluation
  • Ask open-ended questions: 'What would you do next?' not 'Would you check logs?'
  • Draw out quieter participants: 'DevOps team, what's your perspective on this?'
  • Challenge assumptions gently: 'What if that system was also affected?'
  • Don't provide answers - guide participants to discover solutions themselves
  • Take notes on gaps and strong responses for debrief
  • Watch body language - if confusion or disagreement, pause and clarify
  • Keep energy high - DDoS exercises can become repetitive, vary your facilitation style
  • Connect injects to real incidents: 'This actually happened to [Company] in 2023'
  • Validate good ideas: 'That's excellent thinking, let's explore that approach'
  • Redirect tangents: 'That's important, let's table it for debrief and stay focused on containment'

🎬 Closing Script

Thank you all for your engagement today. What we just experienced in 90 minutes could span several hours or days in a real incident - and you&#039;d be exhausted, under pressure, and dealing with incomplete information. You handled this scenario [specific praise based on performance]. Before we wrap, let&#039;s do a quick hot wash: What went well? What was challenging? What surprised you? Then we&#039;ll identify specific gaps and action items to improve our readiness. Remember, the real value of this exercise comes from what we do AFTER - implementing the lessons learned. Let&#039;s make sure we don&#039;t let these insights gather dust.

🔧 Troubleshooting Common Issues

Issue: Team completely stuck, no one responding
Solution: Provide a gentle prompt: 'Let's start with the SOC team - what's the first thing you'd check in your monitoring tools?' or 'What do your runbooks say about handling traffic spikes?'
Issue: One participant dominating discussion
Solution: Politely redirect: 'Thanks John, that's valuable. Let's hear from the DevOps perspective - Sarah, how would you approach this from the infrastructure side?'
Issue: Participants solving by throwing money at it ('We'd just buy Cloudflare Enterprise')
Solution: Acknowledge but constrain: 'That's a valid long-term solution, but assume you need to respond with your current tools and budget. What can you do RIGHT NOW?'
Issue: Technical jargon alienating non-technical participants
Solution: Ask for translation: 'For those less familiar with BGP routing, can someone explain what that means for our game servers?'
Issue: Debate over tool capabilities derailing scenario
Solution: Table it: 'Let's assume for this exercise that [tool] can do X. We'll note this as an action item to verify capabilities afterward.'
Issue: Participants referencing playbooks that don't exist
Solution: Clarify gently: 'Let's assume we don't have that specific playbook yet - that's actually a gap we want to identify. How would you improvise?'

Scenario Overview

Your gaming company operates Rainbow Six: Siege, a popular tactical shooter with millions of active players worldwide. It's Friday evening at 8:00 PM EST - peak gaming hours with over 200,000 concurrent players online. Your infrastructure includes: game servers across 15 regions, centralized authentication/matchmaking services, CDN for asset delivery, and player data APIs. Without warning, your monitoring systems light up with alerts: network traffic has spiked to 500+ Gbps, game servers are unreachable, and players are flooding social media with complaints about connection errors. Your NOC confirms: you're under a massive distributed DDoS attack targeting multiple layers of your infrastructure. The attack is sophisticated, rotating between volumetric floods and application-layer attacks. Player sentiment on Twitter is rapidly turning negative, with #RainbowSixDown trending globally. Your CEO is asking for updates every 15 minutes. The clock is ticking.

04

Attack Chain Timeline

T+0
Initial Attack Detection - Traffic Spike
Network monitoring systems trigger high-severity alerts showing 500+ Gbps inbound traffic spike across all game server regions. Simultaneously, player login failures spike from <1% to 85% within 5 minutes. NOC confirms network saturation on upstream ISP links.
Impact: Immediate player impact: 170,000+ concurrent players disconnected or unable to login. Customer support ticket queue grows from 12 to 400+ in 5 minutes.
🎯 FACILITATOR NOTES - CONFIDENTIAL
📋 Context for Facilitators

This is the classic 'oh no' moment where multiple systems are screaming simultaneously. Watch how the team prioritizes - do they split up to investigate different systems, or do they try to get a unified picture first? Many teams will immediately jump to solutions before fully understanding the scope.

💬 Discussion Prompts
  • Which team takes the lead in the first 5 minutes - SOC, DevOps, or Network?
  • How do you quickly differentiate between a DDoS attack vs. legitimate traffic surge (game patch release, viral streamer)?
  • What's your first priority: stopping the attack, maintaining service, or understanding what's happening?
  • Who makes the decision to enable DDoS mitigation services (if not already enabled)?
  • At what point do you escalate to management vs. attempting to resolve internally first?
📚 Key Teaching Points
  • DDoS attacks often affect multiple systems simultaneously - correlation is key
  • Volumetric attacks (500 Gbps) saturate network infrastructure before reaching application servers
  • Player experience degrades BEFORE systems go fully offline - login failures are early warning
  • Customer support becomes a real-time 'sensor' during player-impacting incidents
  • First 5 minutes set the tone: calm, coordinated response vs. chaotic fire-fighting
T+15
Attack Vector Identification - UDP Amplification
Network analysis reveals UDP amplification attack using NTP and DNS reflection. Attack traffic originates from 15,000+ unique source IPs across 45 countries. ISP upstream mitigation partially activated but attack adapting in real-time, shifting to new reflection methods.
Impact: Partial mitigation reduces traffic to 200 Gbps but login services still degraded. ~50% of players reconnected, but matchmaking queues experiencing 5-10 minute delays instead of typical 30 seconds.
🎯 FACILITATOR NOTES - CONFIDENTIAL
📋 Context for Facilitators

Now the team has more information but the attack is adapting. This tests whether they have playbooks for multi-vector DDoS and whether they understand different mitigation strategies for different attack types. Watch for coordination between network security and game operations teams.

💬 Discussion Prompts
  • Your ISP mitigation is helping but not solving it - what additional layers of defense do you activate?
  • How do you distinguish attack traffic from legitimate player traffic at this scale?
  • Do you implement aggressive rate limiting knowing it might block legitimate players?
  • How are you communicating with players right now - social media, in-game messages, nothing?
  • What data are you collecting for post-incident analysis and potential law enforcement?
📚 Key Teaching Points
  • Sophisticated attackers adapt to mitigation in real-time - expect attack vectors to shift
  • UDP amplification attacks exploit misconfigured public servers (NTP, DNS, SSDP, Memcached)
  • 15,000+ source IPs indicates botnet, not single attacker - blocking IPs individually is futile
  • Partial service restoration creates difficult decisions: risk further disruption with aggressive mitigation?
  • Player communication during degraded service is critical - silence breeds social media anger
T+30
Layer 7 Application Attack Detected
While network team battles volumetric attack, SOC detects secondary application-layer attack: HTTP floods targeting game matchmaking API endpoints. Attack uses legitimate-looking requests with randomized user agents, bypassing rate limits. Authentication service CPU utilization hits 95%, causing cascading delays.
Impact: Matchmaking completely broken despite network stabilization. Players can login but cannot join matches. Effective service outage continues. Social media backlash intensifying with #RainbowSixDown trending at #3 globally.
🎯 FACILITATOR NOTES - CONFIDENTIAL
📋 Context for Facilitators

This is the 'aha' moment where teams realize it's not just a network problem - the application layer is under attack too. Many DDoS playbooks focus only on volumetric attacks. This tests whether teams have visibility into application-layer attacks and can coordinate responses across infrastructure and application teams.

💬 Discussion Prompts
  • Why didn't you detect the Layer 7 attack earlier? What visibility gaps exist?
  • How do you mitigate HTTP floods without blocking legitimate player API requests?
  • Do you implement CAPTCHA challenges knowing it degrades player experience?
  • Your network is stable but the application is crushed - where do you focus resources?
  • Social media is exploding - do you communicate now, or wait until service is restored?
📚 Key Teaching Points
  • Multi-vector attacks intentionally split defender attention across network and application layers
  • Application-layer attacks are harder to mitigate - they look like legitimate traffic
  • CPU exhaustion from processing malicious requests can be as effective as network saturation
  • Gaming APIs have unique challenges: sessions, state, real-time requirements make CAPTCHA difficult
  • Social media monitoring should be part of incident response - it's real-time player sentiment data
T+60
Ransom Demand Received
Anonymous email arrives at security@company.com and CEO's public email. Subject: 'Stop the DDoS - Pay 5 BTC'. Message claims attack will intensify every hour until payment received, threatens to leak player database if payment not received within 24 hours. Includes proof: 1,000 player email addresses and bcrypt hashes from old 2023 breach (previously undisclosed).
Impact: Attack motivation now clear - ransom DDoS. Legal and compliance implications escalate: old breach disclosure requirements, law enforcement notification, ransom payment considerations. CEO demanding immediate executive briefing.
🎯 FACILITATOR NOTES - CONFIDENTIAL
📋 Context for Facilitators

This inject introduces a major complication: it's not just vandalism, it's extortion. The leaked data proves the attackers have prior access (or purchased data from dark web). This tests executive communication, legal coordination, ransom payment policies, and old breach disclosure obligations. Watch for teams that separate the 'stop the DDoS' problem from the 'we need to brief executives' problem.

💬 Discussion Prompts
  • Do you pay the ransom? Who makes that decision? What's your organization's policy?
  • How do you brief the CEO in 5 minutes when you're still fighting the attack?
  • The old breach data - is this a new breach or old data resurfacing? How do you determine that?
  • Do you notify law enforcement? FBI? Secret Service? IC3 report?
  • What changes in your mitigation strategy now that you know it's extortion?
  • Who handles ransom communication - do you respond, ignore, or try to gather intel?
📚 Key Teaching Points
  • Ransom DDoS is increasingly common - attackers threaten, demonstrate capability, then demand payment
  • Paying ransom doesn't guarantee attack cessation - and may mark you as a willing payer
  • Old breach data being used as 'proof' complicates things - is this a new compromise or recycled data?
  • Executive communication during active incidents requires balance: enough detail to inform, not so much they panic
  • Law enforcement notification is complex: FBI for cyber extortion, but response time may not help immediate situation
T+90
CDN Provider Under Attack
Your CDN provider (hosting game assets, patches, store images) calls: they're seeing 800+ Gbps attack traffic targeting your account specifically. They've activated mitigation but warn they may need to temporarily suspend your account to protect other customers if attack continues to escalate. Loss of CDN means players can't download updates or access store.
Impact: Potential loss of CDN means inability to push hotfixes, player asset loading failures, store downtime. Alternative CDN failover untested at this scale. Financial impact: store revenue loss $10K+/hour.
🎯 FACILITATOR NOTES - CONFIDENTIAL
📋 Context for Facilitators

Attackers are now targeting your dependencies - classic DDoS escalation pattern. CDN providers have aggressive abuse policies because one customer's attack impacts many others. This tests whether teams have CDN failover plans, understand blast radius of losing CDN, and can coordinate with third-party vendors during crisis.

💬 Discussion Prompts
  • Do you have a backup CDN provider? How long to failover?
  • Can you operate without CDN? What breaks - just store, or critical game functionality?
  • How do you negotiate with CDN provider - do they have special DDoS mitigation tiers?
  • If CDN suspends your account, how do you communicate that to players?
  • Should you proactively failover to backup CDN before suspension, even if untested?
📚 Key Teaching Points
  • Third-party service providers are often the weakest link in DDoS resilience
  • CDN providers prioritize their infrastructure over individual customers during massive attacks
  • Failover to backup providers should be tested regularly - not during active incident
  • Gaming infrastructure often has hidden CDN dependencies beyond obvious asset delivery
  • Vendor contracts should include DDoS SLAs - but those are often untested until needed
T+120
Attack Subsides - Post-Incident Analysis Begins
After 2 hours, attack traffic drops to near-zero within 5 minutes. All services gradually return to normal. Player reconnections stabilize, matchmaking queues clear. No ransom paid. No additional data leaked. Attack appears to have ended spontaneously. Network forensics team begins analysis of attack patterns, source IPs, and timeline correlation.
Impact: Service restored but player trust damaged. Estimated 20% of active players logged off in frustration. Social media sentiment negative. Financial impact: ~$100K revenue loss (2 hours x $50K/hr), plus reputational damage. Post-incident investigation and hardening required.
🎯 FACILITATOR NOTES - CONFIDENTIAL
📋 Context for Facilitators

The attack stopping suddenly is realistic - many DDoS attacks are time-limited demonstrations. But teams often relax too quickly without considering whether this is truly over or a temporary pause. This tests post-incident processes: forensics, player communication, lessons learned, and preparation for future attacks.

💬 Discussion Prompts
  • Is the attack really over, or just paused? How do you verify?
  • What immediate hardening steps do you take assuming they'll attack again?
  • How do you communicate with players - apologize, explain, compensate with in-game items?
  • What data do you collect for law enforcement and future prevention?
  • Debrief timing - hot wash now while fresh, or let teams rest first?
  • What executive communication happens now - board notification, public statement, press release?
📚 Key Teaching Points
  • DDoS attacks ending abruptly is common - attackers have limited resources or make their point
  • Post-incident hardening window is critical - attackers often return within 24-48 hours
  • Player communication after service restoration can rebuild trust - or destroy it if handled poorly
  • Forensic data collection must happen quickly - logs rotate, memory dumps are lost
  • Real learning happens in post-incident review - block time for deep technical retrospective

Exercise Objectives

Objective 1: Test DDoS Detection and Initial Response

Evaluate the team's ability to rapidly detect DDoS attacks across multiple vectors (volumetric and application-layer), coordinate initial response across SOC/DevOps/Infrastructure teams, and implement first-line mitigation strategies within the critical first 15 minutes of an attack.

Success Criteria:
  • Attack detected within 5 minutes of initial traffic spike through automated monitoring alerts
  • Clear incident commander assigned within 10 minutes with defined roles for each team
  • Initial mitigation (ISP upstream filtering, rate limiting) activated within 15 minutes
  • Team correctly identifies multi-vector attack (volumetric + application layer) within 30 minutes
  • Communication channels established (Slack war room, conference bridge) for coordinated response
🎯 FACILITATOR NOTES - CONFIDENTIAL
🎯 Focus Areas for This Objective
  • Speed of detection - do automated alerts work as expected?
  • Role clarity - does everyone know their responsibilities immediately?
  • Decision-making under pressure - who makes the call to activate expensive mitigation?
  • Cross-team coordination - how do SOC, DevOps, and Network teams communicate?
  • Tooling effectiveness - can teams actually see what's happening quickly?
⚠️ Common Mistakes to Watch For
  • Waiting for complete information before acting - speed matters in DDoS response
  • Each team working in silos without coordinated incident command structure
  • Focusing solely on network layer while application layer burns
  • Over-reliance on single mitigation strategy (e.g., only blocking IPs)
  • Forgetting to loop in Game Operations team who understands player impact
⏱️ Time Guidance

Spend 25-30 minutes on Objectives 1-related injects (T+0 to T+30). This is where most 'process gaps' will be revealed - incident command, runbooks, tool access, communication channels.

Objective 2: Validate Executive Communication and Ransom Decision Framework

Test the team's ability to communicate incident status to executive leadership under time pressure, evaluate organizational policy and decision-making framework for ransom payments, coordinate with legal/compliance on breach disclosure obligations, and manage law enforcement notification procedures.

Success Criteria:
  • Executive briefing delivered within 20 minutes of ransom demand using clear, non-technical language
  • Team correctly identifies data leak as potentially old 2023 breach requiring investigation
  • Ransom payment decision escalated to appropriate authority (CEO, Board) with pros/cons analysis
  • Law enforcement (FBI, IC3) notified within 60 minutes with appropriate details
  • Legal/compliance team engaged for breach notification assessment (GDPR, state laws)
🎯 FACILITATOR NOTES - CONFIDENTIAL
🎯 Focus Areas for This Objective
  • Executive communication quality - can technical teams translate to business impact?
  • Ransom payment policy - does one exist? Is it documented and known?
  • Legal coordination - is legal team part of incident response or afterthought?
  • Law enforcement relationships - do teams know who to call and what to share?
  • Parallel workstreams - can teams continue mitigation while handling executive/legal matters?
⚠️ Common Mistakes to Watch For
  • Trying to fully resolve incident before briefing executives - they need updates, not solutions
  • Not having a pre-defined ransom payment policy forcing real-time ethical debates
  • Treating the data leak as separate from the DDoS - they're parts of same extortion campaign
  • Over-sharing technical details with executives who need business impact summary
  • Assuming law enforcement can't help because attack is overseas
⏱️ Time Guidance

Spend 20-25 minutes on Objective 2 (T+60 inject and discussion). Ransom payment ethics can consume excessive time - keep it focused on organizational policy and decision framework, not philosophical debate.

Objective 3: Evaluate Player Communication and Service Recovery

Assess the team's ability to communicate with affected players during and after the incident, coordinate with customer support and community management teams, implement service recovery procedures (compensation, apologies), and conduct effective post-incident analysis to prevent recurrence.

Success Criteria:
  • Player communication initiated within 30 minutes via Twitter, Discord, in-game messages
  • Community management team provided regular updates throughout incident (every 30-60 min)
  • Post-incident player communication includes transparent explanation and compensation plan
  • Hot wash debrief scheduled within 24 hours while incident details are fresh
  • Action items identified for hardening (CDN failover testing, enhanced DDoS mitigation, playbook updates)
🎯 FACILITATOR NOTES - CONFIDENTIAL
🎯 Focus Areas for This Objective
  • Player communication timing - when is it appropriate to communicate vs. stay silent?
  • Message quality - do teams understand player perspective and appropriate tone?
  • Customer support coordination - how does CS team get info to respond to tickets?
  • Service recovery - what compensation makes sense (refunds, in-game currency, apology)?
  • Post-incident learning - will action items actually get implemented or forgotten?
⚠️ Common Mistakes to Watch For
  • Staying silent too long - players interpret silence as incompetence or not caring
  • Over-promising ('services will be back in 10 minutes') then missing deadlines
  • Generic corporate speak instead of authentic, transparent communication
  • Forgetting that customer support team is flooded and needs talking points
  • Skipping post-incident review because 'we're too busy' - guaranteed repeat performance
⏱️ Time Guidance

Spend 15-20 minutes on Objective 3 (T+90 to T+120 and aftermath). Player communication quality directly impacts retention - this is business-critical, not just PR fluff.

If: What does the packet capture show?

Even if you could block them all, they're spoofed IPs in a reflection attack - the actual attackers aren't in this list. IP blocking is ineffective against this type of DDoS. You need upstream scrubbing or protocol-level mitigation.

  • Team immediately recognizes DDoS attack pattern (not confused about cause)
  • Mention of activating upstream or cloud-based DDoS mitigation within 5 minutes

Scenario

  • Do you track all costs associated with DDoS incidents (mitigation, personnel, compensation)?
  • What does your cyber insurance policy cover for DDoS attacks?
  • What documentation does your insurer require for claims?
  • How do you quantify reputational damage from player-impacting incidents?

  • ADEQUATE: Documented cost tracking methodology, revenue loss calculations, cyber insurance terms understood, claim procedures documented, executive reporting templates, historical cost data for comparison
  • INADEQUATE: Informal cost estimates, incomplete tracking, cyber insurance terms not reviewed, no executive reporting template
  • MISSING: No cost tracking, revenue loss estimates guessed, cyber insurance coverage unknown, no structured reporting
